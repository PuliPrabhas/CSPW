The identification of people preferring for Shopping is an essential part of everyday life, and consumers can choose between online and offline shopping based on their preferences and needs. Offline shopping refers to purchasing products in physical stores, allowing customers to see, touch, and try items before buying. It provides a personalized shopping experience with direct customer service. On the other hand, online shopping offers convenience, a wider selection of products, and the ability to shop anytime from anywhere. Both shopping methods have their advantages and challenges, and consumer choices are influenced by factors such as convenience, product availability, pricing, and personal preferences. In today’s rapidly evolving marketplace, the way consumers choose to shop has undergone a significant transformation. With the proliferation of the internet, smartphones, and digital payment systems, online shopping has emerged as a dominant mode of commerce. At the same time, traditional brick-and-mortar stores continue to serve a substantial portion of the population who still value the in-person shopping experience. The competition and coexistence between online and offline shopping channels have given rise to an important question: What drives consumer preference toward one shopping mode over the other? Understanding these preferences is crucial in the modern retail landscape. Businesses, marketers, and policy makers alike benefit from insights into consumer behavior to tailor services, enhance customer satisfaction, and optimize sales strategies. Some consumers are drawn to the convenience, speed, and product variety that online platforms provide. Others continue to value the tactile, social, and experiential elements of shopping in physical stores. Factors such as age, income, education level, lifestyle, technological access, and even psychological comfort levels influence these choices. Therefore, identifying who prefers which mode of shopping—and why—is not only relevant but essential for the sustainable growth of both online and offline retail sectors. The COVID-19 pandemic further accelerated the adoption of online shopping. Lockdowns, health concerns, and movement restrictions forced many traditional shoppers to explore digital avenues for their daily needs. E-commerce saw record surges in traffic and transactions during this period. However, with the return to post-pandemic normalcy, the world is witnessing a rebalancing act. Some consumers are reverting to in-person shopping, while others have permanently shifted to the digital marketplace. This shift presents a unique opportunity to analyze long-term changes in consumer behavior and preferences. Online shopping, also known as e-commerce, offers multiple advantages. It allows consumers to browse products anytime, compare prices easily, access a wider range of products, and read customer reviews before making a decision. Additionally, online platforms often provide personalized recommendations based on browsing history and prior purchases. However, challenges such as delayed deliveries, inability to physically inspect products, concerns about data security, and complex return policies can deter some shoppers. Conversely, offline shopping—whether in supermarkets, malls, or boutique stores—offers direct interaction with products, immediate gratification through on-the-spot purchases, and personalized customer service. Many consumers find offline shopping more trustworthy and secure, particularly when purchasing high-value or sensitive items such as jewelry, electronics, or luxury goods. For some, the shopping experience itself is social or recreational—a chance to spend time with friends and family or enjoy the ambiance of a physical store. Demographic factors play a significant role in shaping these preferences. For instance, younger generations who have grown up with digital technology may naturally gravitate toward online shopping due to familiarity and ease of use. Older consumers, on the other hand, may be less comfortable with digital interfaces or may prefer the reliability and personal interaction of in-store shopping. Income levels also influence these behaviors—while affluent individuals may have greater access to technology, those with limited resources might still rely on local offline markets. Geographical factors are equally important. Urban residents often have better internet connectivity and access to fast delivery services, which supports the growth of e-commerce. In contrast, rural consumers may face logistical challenges or may lack the infrastructure needed for reliable online shopping. Cultural and regional shopping habits, too, affec toward digital or traditional modes of purchase. Moreover, psychological factors like trust, perceived risk, and the desire for control can strongly impact shopping preferences. Consumers who are more skeptical about product authenticity or online scams may prefer the certainty of in-store transactions. Others might avoid crowds and lines, especially after the pandemic, leading them to favor online channels. Understanding these motivations requires a multidisciplinary approach—drawing from marketing, psychology, sociology, and economics. As technology continues to evolve, hybrid shopping models are emerging. Options like “click and collect,” augmented reality shopping, and virtual try-ons blend the convenience of online shopping with the tangibility of offline experiences. These developments add complexity to the analysis but also open up new opportunities to cater to increasingly diverse consumer needs. This study or discussion on the identification of people who prefer online shopping or offline shopping seeks to uncover these underlying preferences and the rationale behind them. By examining factors such as age, gender, occupation, education, location, and lifestyle, we can develop a clearer profile of online and offline shoppers. Such insights not only help retailers optimize their strategies but also contribute to a broader understanding of consumer behavior in the digital age. The distinction between online and offline shopping is no longer just a matter of preference—it is a reflection of how individuals interact with technology, trust systems, value convenience, and seek experiences. As the retail world becomes increasingly hybridized, identifying who prefers what—and why—remains a vital task for businesses and researchers alike.
